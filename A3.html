Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Question: What is the difference in the age distribution (i.e. population size by age group) by sex when comparing the two years (1900 and 2000)?

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • I wanted to capture total population for each age group by sex and for both years
  • Thought it could be interesting to do a graph similar to a population pyramid but comparing sex between the two years ; having males and females on the same age axis but comparing by year
  • Worked well: showing total population size for each age group and by sex (intuitive); easy to differentiate the total population size with bar chart; good for comparison because the age distribution was on the same axis instead of different charts
  • Didn’t work well: should have made side by side bar chart for men and women for the same year to make easier comparisons between sex; horizontal axis made it more difficult to compare the two years (could have done age axis vertically to compare more easily across all metrics [age and sex and years]); should have included axes labels for population instead of labels on the bars themselves; needed more consistency in positioning of labels
  • Interested in exploring the having all four categories (male 1900, female 1900, male 2000, female 2000) on the same graph to be able to compare population size between sex AND Year more easily

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • I wanted to capture trends in age distribution by sex more easily for each respective year by plotting points for each respective category (male 1900, female 1900, male 2000, female 2000) on one. This allows the points to create a clearer trend pattern to compare each category with the other sex by year categories.
  • Worked well: easy to see the trends between male and female age distribution for each year and for comparison between years (could quickly compare the difference in population size), population on x-axis made it clear what the size of the population per age group was.
  • Didn’t work well: need different color choices (some of the colors were too similar and didn’t really stand out against their counter parts); not super accurate due to x-axis set up (making each major tick a space of 1,000) and due to point size; Age(0-90) going from smallest to largest on the y-axis was kind of difficult to read because the trend is falling.
  • I want to explore the differences in population size between sexes for each age group; basically exploring which sex had a larger population for each age group.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I wanted to capture a different perspective on the trends seen in graphs one and two; I wanted to show the difference in population size between sexes for each respective year and for each age group while having a similar graph set-up as the pseudo population pyramid from sketch 1. I wanted to see which sex had a bigger population per age group in each year.
  • Worked well: transforming the data so that I only had the difference in population size (i.e. how many more males than females or vice versa) helped show trends in age distribution in a novel way; the layout and having the y-axis be age was helpful to compare the two years on either side of the axis; easy to see differences in sex population difference between 1900 and 2000 (how many more of each sex there were by year); good color contrast.
  • Didn’t work well: may not be super intuitive what I mean by population difference, but I also didn’t want to spell it out for the audience (so I included some clarification above the key); age going from 0-90 may not be super conventional for the audience to understand difference over the life course (kind of backwards); don’t see the overall change in population size between the two years.
  • Seeing the difference in population size by sex for each age group was helpful in thinking about why there might be more men than women in certain age groups, and also how this has changed from 1900 to 2000; I would like to further explore the difference in population size of each sex group compared to the total population overtime.

Reflection

When working through the three designs, I quickly found that there were things I liked more about some designs than the others. For Sketch 1 I liked the colors I used to represent sex categories, and how it was a relatively easy graph to read to compare sex categories between the two years, however, there were style choices I would have done differently such as make the age bar be vertical instead of horizontal (so it was more like a population pyramid), and putting the bars for female and male for each age group next to each other to be able to compare more easily for each respective year. The next sketch (dot graph) does a good job of being able to compare the four categories between each other for both population between the same sex over time, but also to compare the difference between the different sexes for the same year. Sketch 2 had some limitations when it came to color choice and accuracy, as well as intuition of making the age go from smallest to largest, but it was more helpful than Sketch 1 in seeing trends in age distribution between the different sexes and comparing between the two years. Sketch 3 was a great way to visualize this idea of difference between sexes for each year and effectively did this through the transformed data (difference between populations of males and females for each age group), the clear contrast in colors, and the layout which mimics a population pyramid but is a nice way to compare the difference in sex category with larger population between the two time periods. However, I wasn’t sure again about how intuitive or well I explained the idea of comparing the difference in population size, nor if making the age (y-axis) 0-90 was also logical for folks, but that does go with the conventions for population pyramids. I want to continue to explore difference in population size compared by sex for each year (which pulls from both Sketch 2 and more specifically Sketch 3) while still being able to see individual age distributions (similar to in Sketch 1 and more so Sketch 2) and being able to compare these between the two time periods.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

For the final graphic I wanted to create a design that showed not only the age distribution trend for males and females respectively, but also a design that allowed for comparison in population size between males and females, and between the two time periods. To answer the question fully, I knew I wanted to take aspects of my part 1 graphs (such as showing the total populations) to see overall trends in age distribution, as well as playing with the formatting to pull out interesting elements (like difference in size between the sex categories). I liked the initial colors I chose to represent men and women since they were bright and contrasted well (without falling into the trope of blue and pink) making it easier to read for the audience, so I wanted to keep that element in my final design. Additionally, I wanted to get age as the x-axis so the potential confusion in logic for age direction for the reader was eliminated; to the same vein, in making the population the y-axis the reader can see the scale of difference in population size better than they could if it were reversed.

I intentionally removed the stacked function for the area plots so that the graphs for age distribution overlaid and so the blue area represents the population size both graphs share, and the difference (which sex had a larger population) peeked out on top. Not only does it emphasize the differences in population size by sex between the two time periods (which shows that women lived much longer compared to men in 2000 than in 1900) but also how these differences changed over time and became much more apparent. These style choices I made such as in the colors and the type of graph make it much more clear the age distribution trend between the two time periods as well. I think a few challenges in this final design were not being able to make a respective population axes for each year so there wasn’t so much room in the 1900 chart (potentially obscuring the data), but this also allows for a better comparison between the difference in population size by sex for each age group between the two years. Additionally, to make it clear to the audience what the blue area in the overlaid area plots represents, I had to annotate the graph which makes it a little less visually appealing in my opinion, but provides a clarity I wouldn’t have been able to get otherwise (which avoids confusion for the audience and potential misunderstandings of the data). I think the age distribution trends by sex are a bit obscured (or at least more difficult to read on first glance) in my final design however, it does show a very clear distinction between the larger population for each age group by sex which is what I wanted to represent.

Reflection

During this process, I felt that my sketches from part 1 had a huge influence on my decision making in part 2. I knew that I wanted to show the full age distribution trend between both years, but after looking at the data in Sketch 2 and noticing some huge differences between the population of males and females, I knew I also wanted to represent the difference between them like I did in Sketch 3. While I didn’t necessarily transform the data manually like I did for Sketch 3 (subtracting one population from the other to get the difference for each age group for that respective year), by overlaying the area plots I was able to visually see the difference between the population of males and females for each age group for each year. Also by playing around with the axes in my previous sketches, I was able to decide what seemed more logical for the audience (especially around how we read ages), and decide how to show the scale of the age distribution more accurately. I think that without prototyping my design, I would have just done something that seemed a bit basic or over-done. By sketching and then creating the final design I was able to decide what data was important for me to show (i.e. general age distribution compared by year) as well as how to show interesting data findings (i.e. difference in male and female populations for each age group by year). I feel like this is a helpful exercise that I plan to continue doing moving forward in my data visualization journey.
